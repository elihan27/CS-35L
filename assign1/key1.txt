6. The two random integers are not truly random.
This is because they're generated by a computer
program, which is deterministic - while they can
produce the illusion of randomness, the machine
relies on the same algorithms and rules every time,
which results in pseudorandom numbers.  

7. The two-variable difference will be incorrect if
the mathematical result is greater than the most
-positive-fixnum or less than the most-negative
-fixnum. It needs to be in that range: that is,
x-y <= most-positive-fixnum and x-y>= most-negative-
fixnum.

Let A = most-positive-fixnum = 2305843009213693951
Let B = most-negative-fixnum = -2305843009213693952
(values of A and B found in lisp)

We can see a pictorial version of this by graphing a
rectangle with corners (B, A), (A, A), (A, B), and
(B, B), then graph both x-y=A and x-y=B.  Those lines
will cut off triangles off the rectangle (which is
actually the region specifiying possible values), and
the area of  what's left of the rectangle will be the
number of differences that stay in range, and thus are
mathematically correct. The area of the triangles will
be the number of differences that are mathematically
incorrect.

Both triangles will have legs |A| and |B|, so the total
area of the triangles would be A*B.

Which means the probability that the difference would
incorrect is:

|A*B|/(A+|B|)^2, (A+|B|)^2 being the possible number of
combinations of x-y.

Assuming my calculator results are correct, this would
be .25, or 1/4.
